The diagnostic problem

One of the most frustrating realities in Lyme disease management is this: serology — the standard diagnostic tool — can oscillate dramatically in the same patient. Positive one month. Negative the next. Borderline the month after.

This isn't lab error. It's biology. Borrelia burgdorferi deploys multiple simultaneous immune evasion strategies: antigenic variation at the VlsE locus, surface protein downregulation during chronic phases, complement inhibition via CRASPs, and intracellular persistence in non-phagocytic cells without triggering cytopathic effects. [1,2] The CDC acknowledges that serologic assays can remain falsely negative for 4–6 weeks post-infection, and that antibody titers cannot confirm cure. [3]

"What if the answer isn't in the blood — but in the heartbeat?"

The ANS as continuous biomarker

The autonomic nervous system doesn't negotiate with pathogens. It responds to them — continuously, measurably, and before symptoms peak.

From my own 87-day longitudinal dataset (Polar Grit X2 + Kubios HRV, Kinetica AI, 2024): ANS suppression was present on approximately 49% of recorded days, with nocturnal RMSSD values clustering in ranges consistent with post-infectious autonomic dysregulation described in ME/CFS and PAIS literature. [4,5] ANS status predicted Post-Exertional Malaise 48 hours ahead (Spearman ρ=+0.431, p=.016, AUC=0.656, sensitivity 81%).

These findings align with a 2025 medRxiv preprint (Tensen et al., n=127 Long COVID patients) demonstrating that wearable HRV monitoring identifies autonomic dysfunction and remains abnormal for 24 hours post-exertion — corroborating the biological plausibility of continuous ANS tracking as an early-warning system in post-acute infectious conditions. [6] A systematic meta-analysis confirms significantly reduced RMSSD in ME/CFS versus controls (SMD −0.37, p=.02). [5]

The clinical implication

When serology oscillates, continuous HRV monitoring provides the temporal correlation that clarifies the picture. A drop in nocturnal RMSSD aligned with a symptomatic flare — replicated across weeks — is biological signal, not noise.

This doesn't replace diagnosis. It informs it.

For a disease that hides this well, we need tools that watch continuously. Not tools that take a single photograph and hope the patient was standing still.

"The nervous system has been tracking Lyme all along. We just weren't listening."
